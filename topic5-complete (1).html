Section 01

Environmental Impact

Computing has a significant environmental footprint across the full lifecycle — from raw material extraction through to disposal.

🏭

Manufacturing

Producing devices uses precious raw materials — rare earth metals, gold, copper — requiring destructive mining. The process consumes significant energy and water, releasing pollutants.

⚡

Energy Consumption

Devices and data centres consume vast electricity. Servers run 24/7, generating heat that requires energy-intensive cooling. Cloud computing shifts this burden to large centralised facilities.

🗑️

e-Waste Disposal

Old devices contain hazardous materials — lead, mercury, cadmium — that can leach into soil and water when improperly disposed of. Developing countries often bear the burden of e-waste processing.

🔄

Replacement Cycles

Frequent device upgrades increase manufacturing demand and e-waste. Extending device lifespans, repairing rather than replacing, significantly reduces environmental harm at both ends of the lifecycle.

☁️

Cloud & Data Centres

Cloud services concentrate energy use in data centres. While more efficient than many on-premises setups, they still account for a major and growing share of global electricity consumption.

♻️

Reducing Impact

Energy-efficient hardware, renewable energy sources, recycling schemes, cloud virtualisation, and "right to repair" legislation can all help reduce computing's environmental footprint.

Key Exam Focus
How do replacement cycles affect the environment? +

Shorter replacement cycles create compounded harm at both ends: more devices are manufactured more frequently (raw materials, energy, water), and more devices are discarded sooner (e-waste). Exam tip: always mention both manufacturing and disposal.

Three stages of environmental impact to remember +
  • Manufacturing — raw material extraction, energy and water use, pollution
  • Use — ongoing electricity consumption, especially data centres
  • Disposal — hazardous materials, e-waste, landfill

A very common mistake is only mentioning one stage. Cover all three for full marks.

Section 02

Ethical & Legal Issues

Understanding the difference between what the law requires and what society considers morally right — and how these apply to computing.

⚖️ Ethics vs Law — the key distinction +

Ethics is about what society considers morally right or wrong. Something can be legal but unethical (e.g. collecting excessive data under broad consent). Legal issues concern what the law requires or prohibits — enforceable by courts. Exam questions often ask you to distinguish these carefully.

🔒 Data Protection Act 2018 / UK GDPR +

Governs how organisations handle personal data. Key principles:

  • Lawfulness & fairness — collected legally and transparently
  • Purpose limitation — only used for the stated purpose
  • Data minimisation — only collect what is necessary
  • Accuracy — kept up to date
  • Storage limitation — not kept longer than needed
  • Integrity & confidentiality — kept secure
  • Accountability — organisations are responsible for compliance
💻 Computer Misuse Act 1990 +

Makes it illegal to access computer systems without authorisation. Three offences:

  • Section 1 — Unauthorised access to computer material (basic hacking)
  • Section 2 — Unauthorised access with intent to commit a further offence
  • Section 3 — Unauthorised modification of computer material (e.g. malware)
🕵️ Privacy & Personal Data +
  • Consent — people should know what data is collected and agree to it
  • Data ownership — who really "owns" your personal data?
  • Surveillance — governments and companies can track individuals
  • Profiling — detailed profiles built for targeted advertising
  • Right to be forgotten — individuals can request data deletion
🌐 Impact on Society +
  • Job automation displaces workers but creates new industries
  • The digital divide creates inequality in access to technology
  • Remote working became possible at scale because of computing
  • Social media changed communication but enables misinformation to spread rapidly
  • Always-connected devices raise questions about wellbeing and attention
Section 03

Intellectual Property

Protecting creative and technical works through copyright, patents, trademarks, and licensing — including the open source model.

©️

Copyright

Automatically protects original creative works — code, music, images, text. No registration needed. Prevents unauthorised copying, distributing, or adapting without permission.

🏛️

Patents

Protect new inventions and technical innovations. Must be registered. Grant exclusive rights for ~20 years in exchange for public disclosure of how the invention works.

™️

Trademarks

Protect brand names, logos, and symbols that distinguish a company's products. Must be registered. Can be renewed indefinitely as long as the mark remains actively in use.

📄

Licensing

Legal agreements defining how software may be used — number of installations, modifications permitted, commercial use. Types: commercial, freeware, shareware, open-source.

🆓

Open Source

Source code is freely available for anyone to view, modify, and distribute. Examples: Linux, Python, Firefox. Encourages collaboration but raises questions about attribution and accountability.

🚫

Piracy & Plagiarism

Using software without a valid licence (piracy) or copying others' work without credit (plagiarism) are both illegal under copyright law and deprive creators of compensation.

Section 04

AI, Machine Learning & Robotics

The ethical, legal, and social implications of intelligent and automated systems — from algorithmic bias to autonomous vehicles.

🤖 What is AI in this context? +

Artificial Intelligence refers to systems that perform tasks normally requiring human intelligence. Machine Learning is a subset where systems learn patterns from data rather than following explicit rules. Robotics combines AI with physical machines that interact with the world.

⚠️ Ethical Issues with AI +
  • Bias — AI trained on biased data produces biased outputs (e.g. facial recognition less accurate for darker skin tones)
  • Accountability — if an AI makes a harmful decision, who is legally responsible?
  • Transparency — many AI systems are "black boxes" whose decisions can't be explained
  • Job displacement — automation threatens many manual and routine cognitive jobs
  • Autonomy — at what point should machines make life-or-death decisions?
🚗 Autonomous Vehicles +
  • The trolley problem — if a crash is unavoidable, should the car protect its passenger or minimise total harm?
  • Liability — if a self-driving car causes an accident, who is at fault: owner, manufacturer, or programmer?
  • Reliability — computers may be faster than humans in many situations but fail in unpredictable edge cases
  • Public trust — acceptance requires a long track record of demonstrated safety
🏥 AI in Healthcare & Other Sectors +
  • Medical imaging AI can detect conditions faster than doctors — but who is responsible for misdiagnoses?
  • Benefits: consistency, speed, availability (no fatigue), lower cost
  • Risks: lack of empathy, over-reliance, incorrect decisions in edge cases
  • In law, finance, and recruitment, AI decisions can seriously affect people's lives
🔐 AI and Data Privacy +
  • AI requires huge training datasets, often including personal data
  • Facial recognition in public spaces enables mass surveillance
  • Social media AI creates "filter bubbles" by prioritising aligned content
  • Deepfakes can impersonate real people, enabling misinformation and fraud

Ethics

GDPR / DPA 2018
UK law governing how organisations must collect, store and use personal data.
Copyright
Automatic legal protection for original creative work; prevents unauthorised copying.
Patent
Registered protection for a new invention, granting exclusive rights for ~20 years.
Trademark
Registered symbol, word or logo that identifies a brand's products or services.
Licence
Legal permission to use software or intellectual property under specific terms.
Open Source
Software whose source code is freely available for anyone to view, modify and share.
e-waste
Discarded electronics, often containing hazardous materials such as lead and mercury.
Replacement Cycle
How often users upgrade devices; shorter cycles increase manufacturing demand and e-waste.
Machine Learning
A type of AI that learns patterns from data rather than following explicitly programmed rules.
AI Bias
Unfair AI outputs caused by training on biased or unrepresentative data.
Digital Divide
Inequality in access to and ability to use digital technology across different groups.

Informed agreement before personal data is collected or used by an organisation.

1990 UK law making unauthorised access to computer systems illegal.

Who bears legal and moral responsibility when an AI system causes harm.
Stakeholder
Anyone directly or indirectly affected by, or with an interest in, a technology or issue.

GDPR principle: only collect personal data strictly necessary for the stated purpose.

GDPR principle: personal data must only be used for the purpose it was collected for.
Section 06

Exam Tips

What examiners are looking for — and the most common mistakes students make in Topic 5 questions.

Structure & Approach
🎯 Exam Structure

Topic 5 is Question 5 on Paper 1, worth ~10–15 marks. Questions range from 2-mark definitions to 6-mark extended responses requiring multiple perspectives and stakeholders.

💡 Extended Responses

For 4–6 mark questions: state the issue, explain why it matters, give a specific example, identify who is affected (stakeholders), and end with a balanced conclusion.

📌 Mark Scheme Tips

Examiners award marks for distinct points. Avoid repeating the same idea. Aim for breadth — one clear point per mark — rather than one long explanation for all marks.

Common Mistakes
⚠️ Ethics vs Legal

Confusing ethical and legal issues loses marks every year. Ask: is this about what the law requires (legal) or what is morally right or wrong (ethical)?

⚠️ Environmental — One Stage

Students often only mention energy use. Cover all three stages: manufacturing, use (energy), and disposal (e-waste). Replacement cycles link manufacturing and disposal together.

⚠️ IP — Wrong Type

Matching wrong protection to wrong IP type loses easy marks. Remember: source code → copyright; new invention → patent; brand logo → trademark; permission to use → licence.

Topic-Specific Advice
💡 AI Questions

For AI ethics, always address bias, accountability, and inability to handle novel situations. Autonomous vehicles are a favourite — know the ethical dilemmas well.

💡 Data Protection

Use correct GDPR terminology: "data minimisation", "purpose limitation". Apply the principle directly to the scenario given in the question for maximum marks.

🎯 Stakeholders

Go beyond the obvious. For a data breach consider: customers (privacy), employees (reputation), shareholders (financial), regulators (legal), wider public (trust).
